Pre‑Move Checklist MUST DRILL

Run these 3 steps before every single move — this alone beats 80% of 1300 blunders.

1. Safety — “Is my piece hanging?”

  • Scan every loose piece (queen, rooks, minor pieces). Count attackers vs. defenders.
  • If a piece is attacked as many times as it is defended, do not move until you reinforce or trade.
  • Pay special attention to your back‑rank — if your king is trapped behind pawns, a rook on the 8th can be deadly.

2. Threats — “What is their last move attacking?”

  • Identify the new square their piece just moved to. Did it open a diagonal for a bishop? Is it attacking your queen?
  • Ask: “Is my opponent threatening mate in 1 or a winning fork?” Always respond to the biggest threat first.
  • If they attack your queen, consider a counter‑attack with check — don’t always retreat!

3. Blunder‑check — “Does my move leave something unprotected?”

  • After you mentally make the move, re‑evaluate: does it remove a defender from your king? Does it block your rook’s line?
  • Never move a piece if it unpins an enemy piece that was trapped — unless you gain a bigger advantage.
  • The golden rule: If you see a good move, look for a better one. Pause 3 more seconds.
Pro tip: Whisper these three out loud as you look at the board. It forces your brain into structured mode.

Opening Repertoire Deep lines

Pick one for White and one for Black. Master them cold — no need for 20 openings.

White · London System (Solid & reliable)

Core moves:

1. d4
2. Bf4
3. e3
4. Nf3
5. Be2
6. 0‑0
7. c3
8. Nbd2
  • Why? Avoids sharp theory. Bishop on f4 is active, pawns on e3/c3 give a rock‑solid center.
  • If Black plays …c5, you can take and recapture with the pawn — keep the structure intact.
  • Typical plan: push e4 (after proper preparation) to break the center.

Black vs e4 · Caro‑Kann (Ultra‑solid)

Core moves:

1. e4 c6
2. d4 d5
3. exd5 cxd5
4. Bd3 Nc6
5. c3 Nf6
6. Bf4 Bg4
  • Why? You get a pawn on d5 that exchanges their center. Very few traps.
  • Develop the bishop to f5 or g4 to put pressure on their knight.
  • Castle short quickly, then play …e6 and …Bd6 to trade off their active bishop.

Black vs d4 · King’s Indian Setup

Core moves:

1. d4 Nf6
2. c4 g6
3. Nc3 Bg7
4. e4 d6
5. Be2 0‑0
6. Nf3 e5
  • Why? Fianchetto gives king safety and a powerful bishop on the long diagonal.
  • Strike in the center with …e5 to challenge their pawn chain.
  • Typical plan: build a kingside attack with …Ne8‑f6‑g5 and …f5.
Golden rule: Castle before move 10. Don’t bring the queen out before move 10. Never move the same piece twice in the opening unless forced.

Middlegame Tactics Bank

This is where games are won. Learn these 4 tactical patterns by heart.

Fork · One piece attacks two

  • Knight fork: Nc7+ forks the king and rook. Ne7+ forks king and queen.
  • Pawn fork: d4‑d5 can attack a knight and bishop simultaneously.
  • Tip: Always check if your opponent’s pieces are on the same colour square as your knight.

Pin · Immobilise a defender

  • Absolute pin: a piece is in front of the king — it cannot move at all (e.g., Bg5 pinning a knight to the queen).
  • Relative pin: in front of the queen or rook — moving it loses material, but it’s not illegal.
  • Pins are deadly in the middlegame — pile up attackers on a pinned piece to win it.

Skewer · The bigger fish

  • Attack a valuable piece (king/queen) so that when it moves, a less valuable piece behind it is captured.
  • Classic: rook on the same file as their king and queen — the king must move, then you take the queen.
  • Bishops on a diagonal can also skewer rooks or queens.

Back‑rank mate · The silent killer

  • If the king is trapped behind pawns on g2/h2 or g7/h7 (no escape squares), a rook or queen on the 8th (or 1st) rank delivers mate.
  • Before you castle, always think: “Do I need luft?” — push h3 or a3 to give your king an escape.
  • In the middlegame, look for rook lifts to the 2nd or 7th rank to pressure the back rank.
Counter‑attack principle: When they attack your piece, look for a bigger threat first — a check or mate that forces them to respond.

Endgame · Converting Wins

Most 1200‑1300 players reach a winning endgame and throw it. Lock these in.

The 50/50 rule

  • If you are up a pawn: trade pieces (queens, rooks) but never trade pawns — keep your extra pawn alive.
  • If you are down a pawn: trade pawns to remove their advantage, and keep pieces on to complicate.

King activation

  • In the endgame, the king is a fighting piece. Walk it toward the center to support pawns.
  • Rule of thumb: the king should be on the same side as your passed pawn to escort it.

Rook + King mate (box method)

  • Use the rook to cut off the enemy king on a rank or file.
  • Bring your king closer to support the rook.
  • Deliver checks along the rank/file, shrinking the box until mate.

Queen + King mate

  • Place your queen a knight’s move away from their king — this restricts all escape squares.
  • Copy their king’s moves until it is pushed to the edge, then bring your king to deliver the final mate.

Passed pawns

  • Put your rook behind the passed pawn (on its file) to push it up the board.
  • If you have two passed pawns separated by one file, they are unstoppable — use them as a battering ram.
Endgame motto: “Passed pawns must be pushed.” And never rush — take your time to calculate.
