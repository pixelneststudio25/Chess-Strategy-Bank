Pre‑Move Checklist MUST DRILL

Say these 3 steps out loud before every single move – they stop 80% of 1300‑level blunders.

1. Safety – “Is any of my pieces left hanging?”

  • Look at your queen, rooks, and minor pieces. For each one, ask: “How many enemy pieces are attacking it, and how many of mine are defending it?”
  • If the attackers outnumber the defenders, do not move anything else until you protect that piece or trade it away.
  • Special attention: if your king is stuck behind its own pawns on the back row, an enemy rook or queen might deliver a sudden mate – give your king an escape square by pushing a side pawn.

2. Threats – “What did my opponent just attack?”

  • Find the piece your opponent just moved. Ask: “What new square is it attacking? Does it open a line for their bishop or queen?”
  • Ask: “Are they threatening to checkmate me in one move, or win my queen with a fork?” Always deal with the biggest threat first.
  • If they attack your queen, don’t panic and run – look for a counter‑attack that gives check. Sometimes the best defence is to attack back!

3. Blunder‑check – “Does my move accidentally leave something behind?”

  • Picture your move in your head. Now re‑evaluate: does moving that piece remove a defender from your king? Does it block your rook’s line of sight?
  • Never move a piece if it unpins an enemy piece that was trapped – unless you win something even bigger.
  • Golden rule: if you spot a good move, wait three seconds and look for an even better one.
Pro tip: Whisper “Safety – Threats – Blunder” as you look at the board. This forces your brain into a disciplined routine.

Opening Repertoire Simple steps

Pick one for White and one for Black. Learn these in plain steps – no memorising long letter‑number sequences.

White · The London System (rock‑solid)

Your moves in order:

1. Push the pawn in front of your queen two squares forward.
2. Bring your king’s bishop out to the square right next to your queen’s knight (pointing diagonally toward their king side).
3. Push the pawn in front of your king one square forward.
4. Bring your king’s knight out to the square just in front of your king’s bishop’s pawn.
5. Develop your king’s bishop to the square directly in front of your king (or next to it).
6. Castle king‑side (tuck your king behind the three pawns on the right).
7. Push the pawn in front of your queen’s bishop one square forward.
8. Bring your queen’s knight out to the square in front of your queen’s bishop’s pawn.
  • Why? This setup avoids wild traps. Your bishop is active, and your center pawns form a wall that’s hard to break.
  • If your opponent attacks your center with their pawn, you can capture and recapture – keep your structure intact.
  • Your typical plan: later, push your king’s pawn one more square to open up the center when you’re ready.

Black vs e4 · The Caro‑Kann (super safe)

Your moves in order (when they open with the king’s pawn):

1. They push their king’s pawn two squares – you answer by pushing the pawn in front of your queen’s bishop one square forward.
2. They push their queen’s pawn two squares – you push your queen’s pawn two squares forward.
3. They capture your queen’s pawn with theirs – you recapture with your pawn.
4. Develop your king’s bishop to the square just in front of your queen’s knight.
5. Push the pawn in front of your queen’s bishop one square forward to support your center.
6. Bring your king’s knight out to the square in front of your king’s bishop’s pawn.
7. Bring your queen’s bishop out to the square just in front of your queen’s rook’s pawn (attacking their knight).
  • Why? You get a pawn on your queen’s file that exchanges their center pawn. Very few tricks against this.
  • Develop your queen’s bishop to either the square in front of your queen’s rook’s pawn or the square in front of your queen’s knight to put pressure on their pieces.
  • Castle king‑side quickly, then push your king’s pawn one square and trade off their active bishop.

Black vs d4 · King’s Indian Setup

Your moves in order (when they open with the queen’s pawn):

1. They push their queen’s pawn two squares – you bring your king’s knight out to the square in front of your king’s bishop’s pawn.
2. They push their queen’s bishop’s pawn two squares – you push your king’s bishop’s pawn one square forward.
3. They bring their queen’s knight out – you develop your king’s bishop to the square in front of your king’s rook’s pawn (fianchetto).
4. They push their king’s pawn two squares – you push your queen’s bishop’s pawn one square forward.
5. Develop your king’s bishop to the square in front of your king (or next to it).
6. Castle king‑side.
7. Bring your king’s knight out – then push your king’s pawn two squares forward to challenge their center.
  • Why? The bishop on the long diagonal is a monster – it gives you great king safety and attacks their center.
  • Strike in the center by pushing your king’s pawn two squares to challenge their pawn chain.
  • Typical plan: build up an attack on their king side by bringing your knight around and pushing your king’s bishop’s pawn.
Golden rule: Get your king safe by castling before move 10. Never bring your queen out early. And don’t move the same piece twice in the opening unless you absolutely have to.

Middlegame Tactics Bank

These 4 patterns win more games than anything else. Learn to see them in your sleep.

Fork – One piece attacks two enemies at once

  • Knight fork: Move your knight so it attacks their king and their rook at the same time – they have to save the king, and you take the rook.
  • Pawn fork: Push a pawn forward so it attacks both a knight and a bishop simultaneously.
  • Tip: Knights attack the opposite colour square they stand on – so if their king and queen are on the same colour, your knight might be able to fork them!

Pin – Freeze a defender in place

  • Absolute pin: If you line up a bishop or rook so that the piece in front is shielding the king, that piece cannot legally move – because that would expose the king to check.
  • Relative pin: If the shielded piece is in front of the queen or a rook – it can move, but you’ll lose your queen if you do, so it’s stuck practically.
  • Pins are deadly: pile up more attackers onto a pinned piece – you’ll win it eventually.

Skewer – Attack the big fish to catch the small one behind

  • Line up your rook or bishop so it attacks their king (or queen). When they move that valuable piece out of the way, you capture the less valuable piece that was sitting behind it.
  • Classic example: your rook on the same file as their king and queen – you give check, their king moves, then you take their queen.
  • Bishops can also skewer rooks or queens along a diagonal.

Back‑rank mate – The silent killer

  • If your opponent’s king is trapped on the back row with no escape squares (pawns still in front of it), a rook or queen on that back row delivers checkmate.
  • Prevention: Before you castle, ask: “Do I need an escape square?” – push a side pawn (like the one in front of your rook) so your king can slip out if needed.
  • In the middlegame, look to lift your rook to the second or seventh rank to pressure their back row.
Counter‑attack principle: When they attack your piece, don’t always retreat. Look for a bigger threat – a check or a mate that forces them to respond to you first.

Endgame · Converting Wins

Most 1200‑1300 players reach a winning endgame and throw it away. Lock these rules in.

The 50/50 rule

  • If you are up by one pawn: trade pieces (queens, rooks) but never trade pawns – keep that extra pawn alive to become your winning ticket.
  • If you are down by a pawn: trade pawns to remove their advantage, but keep pieces on the board to create complications.

King activation

  • In the endgame, your king is no longer a sheltered piece – it becomes a fighting warrior. Walk it toward the center to support your pawns.
  • Rule of thumb: your king should be on the same side as your passed pawn to escort it all the way.

Rook + King mate (the box method)

  • Use your rook to cut off their king on a row or column – like a barrier.
  • Bring your king closer to help the rook.
  • Give checks along that row or column, shrinking the box smaller and smaller until it’s mate.

Queen + King mate

  • Place your queen one square diagonally away from their king (like a knight’s jump away) – this covers all the escape squares.
  • Copy their king’s moves – if they go up, you go up; if they go left, you go left – until they are pushed to the edge, then bring your king to deliver the final mate.

Passed pawns

  • Put your rook behind your passed pawn (on the same file, behind it) to push it forward relentlessly.
  • If you have two passed pawns with one file between them, they are unstoppable – use them like a battering ram.
Endgame motto: “Passed pawns must be pushed.” And never rush – take your time to calculate each move carefully.
